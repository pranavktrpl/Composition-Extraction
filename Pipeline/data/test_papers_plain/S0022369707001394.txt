Thin TiSiN flat films deposited by MOCVD have been analysed in order to evaluate their microstructure and physical properties. TEM, XRR and RBS techniques have been utilised as complementary analyses to evaluate thickness, density and composition. The main composition is Ti : N : Si = 0.9 : 1.1 : 0.14   with carbon and oxygen up to 5% as a result of organic residuals and oxidation. The analysis shows a layered structure which well correlates with deposition steps. TiN nanocrystals embedded in a SiN  x    -rich amorphous matrix result confined into each TiSiN deposited layer. The silane soak produces a low concentration silicon inclusion, mainly stabilising the grain boundaries and not affecting the TiN crystalline morphology. AES profiles show that the TiSiN film consists of Ti–N rich layers, separated by C and Si rich “interface” layers which are the result of the process sequence. Densification and crystallisation of the film were observed after high temperature annealing in vacuum, resulting in an electrical resistivity decrease. PECVD deposition of an oxide layer on TiSiN causes a strong oxidation and resistivity increase of the film.   Keywords A. Thin films  D. Microstructure    1 Introduction TiSiN is a composite material developed during the early 1980s [1] . The main studies have been focused on its enhanced hardness and refractory properties produced by the nanocomposite microstructure of TiN nanocrystals embedded in an amorphous Si  3   N  4     matrix. The application interest was as hard coating layer for wear protection. More recently TiSiN has been considered to be used in IC technology, mainly as diffusion barrier [2] . Silicon content affects the physical properties of the film like electrical resistivity [2,3] , oxidation stability [4–6]  and diffusion barrier efficiency [7,8] . TiSiN is a refractory material candidate to be used in phase change memory as contact electrode to the calchogenide material, owing to its high temperature stability. For this application, the severe thermal operation conditions can set a big constrain on the material selection.  2 Experimental The TiSiN deposition process utilised in this study consists of TiN deposition by MOCVD (metal-organic chemical vapour deposition) followed by an annealing at 400   ∘   C   in SiH  4     flow to promote Si diffusion and reaction. MOCVD TiN deposition is produced by thermal decomposition of tetrakis-dimethylamino-titanium (TDMAT) followed by a H  2   / N  2     plasma treatment at 400   ∘   C   to remove the organic residue and to promote film densification. TiN layer must be sufficiently thin to ensure plasma efficiency, Si diffusion and reaction inside the whole film during the soak and to produce a more homogenous composition. Thicker films can be obtained by multiple TiSiN layer deposition. The analyses have been performed on 3 × 5  nm   and 4 × 5  nm   (nominal thickness) TiSiN films deposited on a CVD-deposited SiO  2     layer, 120–150 nm thick, on silicon substrate. After deposition thermal treatments were applied to study possible TiSiN modification during subsequent processing steps: rapid thermal annealing (RTA) at 715   ∘   C   in N  2     for 60 s and furnace annealing at 800 or 900   ∘   C   in vacuum (p < 2 × 10  - 7    mbar  , heating/cooling 7   ∘   C / min  ). To investigate the effect of exposure to an oxidising ambient a SiO  2     layer of 60 nm has been deposited by plasma-enhanced chemical vapour deposition (PECVD-TEOS) at 550   ∘   C   on some TiSiN ( 3 × 5  nm )   films. A furnace annealing at 800   ∘   C   in air was also tested. The structure of the films was characterised by transmission electron microscopy (TEM) with a LEO-922, operating at 200 kV, and by X-ray diffractometry (XRD) using an Italstructure HRD 3000 diffractometer with Cu K α   radiation and a curved position-sensitive detector over a range of 120  ∘     in 2 θ  . The system is equipped with a multilayer monochromator–collimator. The composition of the films was investigated by Auger electron spectroscopy (AES), X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) and Rutherford backscattering spectrometry (RBS). AES depth profiles were obtained by a PHI 670 system. XPS analyses were performed using Al K α   radiation. RBS measurements were performed using a He  ++     beam at the energy of 1.5 MeV with backscattering angle π – ϕ = 160  ∘     and tilt angle θ = 45  ∘    . The spectra were analysed with the RUMP simulation code for quantification of stoichiometry. Specular X-ray reflectivity (XRR) spectra were collected with a single point detector. A slit defines the dimensions of the collimated beam to 6  mm × 100  μ m  . Data are acquired in θ – 2 θ   mode. Thickness, roughness and density of the layers were obtained using a model based on the Matrix formalism [9] . TEM, XRR, and RBS techniques have been utilised as complementary analyses to evaluate thickness, density and composition: the thickness values from TEM are the references for XRR data fitting, the refined thickness and electron density values from XRR are used as crossing checking point for RBS results on elemental composition.  3 Results and discussion Owing to the multistep process a multilayered structure is expected. TEM cross section analysis shows a layered structure with higher density layers alternated to less dense ones (Fig. 1  ). The higher density layer consists of nanocrystals smaller than 5 nm along the film thickness (darker regions in Fig. 1 ) embedded in an amorphous matrix, “confined” into each TiSiN single layer. The results are consistent with literature data of multilayer TiSiN films deposited by a CVD process similar to what used in this study [10] . The thickness of the layers is uniform, apart that of the first layer which has been grown on a different substrate (amorphous SiO  2    ) compared to the subsequent ones. The analysis of TEM plan view on 4 × 5  nm   TiSiN film indicates the presence of polycrystalline grains in the whole film with lateral dimensions smaller than 10 nm. Electron diffraction image from TEM plan view shows diffraction rings corresponding to TiN polycrystals with lattice parameter a = 0.42  nm   (Fig. 2  ). No significant differences were found after RTA at 715   ∘   C   by TEM cross section and plan view, as well as by all the other techniques we utilised. From XRD measurements performed in grazing angle configuration (incidence angle: ω = 1  ∘    ) on 4 × 5  nm   nominal thickness TiSiN films, diffraction peaks corresponding to TiN crystal diffraction lines have been detected (Fig. 3  ): NaCl structure type; cell parameter a = 0.423  nm  . Furnace annealing in vacuum at 900   ∘   C   for 1 min (7   ∘   C / min   ramps) produces further crystallisation. The peaks line-width is large as indication of nanometric grains. The Auger depth profile for a 4 × 5  nm   TiSiN layer, shown in Fig. 4  , clearly reveal that each deposited TiSiN layer consists of two sublayers; the thicker sublayers are Ti–N rich, separated by “interface” layers rich in C and Si which are the result of the process sequence: the H  2   / N  2     plasma removes a large portion of the organic residue but is only effective to a depth of 3–5 nm, leaving more carbon in the bottom of each TiN layer; silane is more effective on the exposed surface. The combination of this two process steps (relative to different TiSiN layer deposition cycles) produces a higher concentration of Si and C at each interface between “single” TiSiN layers. XPS analysis around Si 2 p   and Ti 2 p   lines (Fig. 5  ) indicates that at the surface of the TiSiN film both Ti and Si are oxidised (Ti 2 p  3 / 2   = 458  eV  ; Si 2 p = 102.5  eV  [11] ) while binding energies corresponding to Ti–N and Si–N atomic bond (Ti 2 p  3 / 2   = 455.3  eV  ; Si 2 p = 101  eV  [11,12] ) were measured as main components in the bulk of the film. XRR data have been fitted with calculated reflectivity pattern from a simulated layered structure, the results for the 4 × 5  nm   film are shown in Fig. 6  . TEM data on sublayers’ thickness are used as guess and check values. The film consists of N  couples of layers corresponding to each N × 5  nm   deposited TiSiN layer. The calculated thickness of each couple is 5–5.5 nm. Each couple has a sub-layer structure which can be modelled with two layers with different electron density of 0.50 ± 0.05   and 1.31 ± 0.04  e / A  ˚    3     and thickness of 1.3–1.7 and 3.7–3.8 nm, respectively. The roughness of each sublayer results in 0.7–1.5 nm. Since the electron density of the high density sublayer is lower than the value corresponding to full dense crystalline TiN ( 1.54  e / A  ˚    3   )   the results are consistent with a sublayer composed of crystalline TiN embedded into a matrix with lower density: amorphous TiN  x     and/or SiN  y    . As noticed also by TEM analysis, the electron density profiles of the first deposited layer has a larger “effective” thickness because of the SiO  2   / TiSiN   interface with electron density of 0.85 ( 7 )  e / A  ˚    3    , which enlarges the effective thickness of the sublayer with higher density (insets in Fig. 6 ). This feature can be related to different, substrate dependent, reaction/growing mechanisms. Furnace annealing in vacuum at 900   ∘   C   for 1 min produces in 4 × 5  nm   TiSiN films an increasing of the electron density of the sublayers with higher density, up to 1.41 ± 0.04  e / A  ˚    3    , compared to the as-deposited film (Table 1  ), consistent with an enhanced TiN crystallisation as revealed also by XRD analysis. The increasing of the critical wave vector q  c     and the widening of the Kiessig fringes support this consideration. Moreover, the annealing induces a decreasing of the electron density of the SiO  2   / TiSiN   interface layer to a value of 0.65 ± 0.05  e / A  ˚    3    , which can be the result of an interface reaction (SiO  2     reduction by Ti, TiSi  x     formation, etc.). Since RBS is not able to resolve the sublayer structure of N × 5  nm   TiSiN films the correlation between RBS spectra modelling and XRR data has been made between atomic density and electron density averaged along the whole TiSiN film thickness. Using the measured TiSiN total thickness (from TEM and/or XRR) as input constant, the variables for RBS spectra modelling are the elemental composition and the atomic density. The correct elemental composition is extracted by the best data fitting which utilises as atomic density the value calculated by the electron density and the composition itself by ρ  a   = ρ  e   ∑  i    a  i    ∑  i    a  i   f  i     ,    where ρ  a     is the average atomic density ( at / cm  3   )  , ρ  e     is the average electron density ( e / cm  3   )  , a  i     the number of i  -atom in the molecular formula and f  i     the real part of the atomic scattering factor for the i  -atom. In Fig. 7   are plotted the experimental and the simulated RBS spectra of a 4 × 5  nm   TiSiN film. The simulation is consistent with the following stoichiometry: Ti : Si : N = 0.9 ( ± 0.1 ) : 0.14 ( ± 0.05 ) : 1.1 ( ± 0.1 )  , i.e. Si relative content is around 6%. The corresponding atomic density of the TiSiN film which gives the best solution is 8.3 × 10  22    at / cm  3    , in good agreement with the electron density value from XRR, averaged along the film. Literature RBS data [2]  indicate values of 8.0 – 10.5 × 10  22     and 7.6 – 9.3 × 10  22    at / cm  3     for TiN  x     and SiN  x     compounds, respectively. The simulation is consistent also with a content of about 5% of C and O, as detected also by XPS and AES analysis. Electrical sheet resistance has been measured by four-point probe Van der Pauw technique on as-deposited films and after annealing at different temperatures and atmospheres. The samples have square shape ( 20  mm × 20  mm )   and the contacts are positioned at their vertexes. The results are summarised in Table 2  . The sheet resistance of a TiSiN ( 4 × 5  nm )   film decreases no more than 5% after RTP annealing at 715   ∘   C   in N  2    , while a furnace annealing in vacuum at 800   ∘   C / 10 min   produces a decreasing of about 30%. The result is consistent with an enhanced film crystallisation/densification compared to the as-deposited one. 3.1 Effect of SiO  2     deposition The deposition of SiO  2     on a TiSiN film produces a modification of the TiSiN layers more directly exposed to the O  3     plasma. In Fig. 8   the TEM image of a 3 × 5  nm   TiSiN layer after SiO  2     deposition is shown. As a result the multilayer structure is less definite and the top part of the film appears lighter compared to films without SiO  2     top layer. The features are consistent with film oxidation. In Fig. 9   the XRR results are shown: the roughness rises to a value of 2–2.5 nm compared to the as-deposited film, which hinders a clear identification of single TiSiN layers. The convoluted electron density in the inset of Fig. 9  shows a decreasing of the electron density in the whole film, especially near the surface exposed to the O  3     plasma of the TEOS deposition process. As a result the TiSiN film total thickness increases of about 10–20% compared to the as-deposited one. As a comparison an oxidation has been performed in air at 800   ∘   C   for 10 min on a 3 × 5  nm   TiSiN film: the X-ray reflectivity pattern indicates that the total thickness increases as in the previous case but the oxidation results more homogenous along the thickness and the layers appear still definite. A slight decrease of the sublayer density is observed, as reported in Table 1 . The deposition of SiO  2     produces a strong increase of the electrical resistivity up to about 1.5  m Ω  cm  . The effect is consistent with the observed film oxidation.   4 Conclusions Thin TiSiN flat films deposited by CVD have been analysed in order to evaluate their microstructure and physical properties. TEM, XRR and RBS techniques have been utilised as complementary analyses to evaluate thickness, density and composition. The mean composition is Ti : N : Si = 0.9 : 1.1 : 0.14   with carbon and oxygen up to 5% as a result of organic residuals and oxidation. The analysis shows a layered structure which well correlates with deposition steps. TiN nanocrystals embedded in an amorphous matrix result confined into each TiSiN deposited layer. The interfaces between adjacent layers have lower density, consistent with Si- and C-rich regions. Titanium and silicon are mainly bonded to nitrogen, as shown by XPS. Si solubility in TiN should be very low because of different ionic radius and chemical nature of Ti ( Ti  3 +   = 0.75  A  ˚   )   and Si ( Si  4 +   = 0.41  A  ˚   )  . As a consequence, in the case of Ti–Si–N compounds, the microstructure consists of TiN  x     and SiN  y     regions and the coalescence of TiN crystals is controlled by the amorphous SiN  y    . The majority of the studies on “mictamic” (mixing of immiscible) materials was made by co-sputtering deposition of transition elements in nitriding or oxidising atmosphere, so that both compounds are formed at the same time improving an intimate mixing. This is not the case for TiSiN MOCVD process, where silicon inclusion is completed after TiN deposition. Silane soak is not able to break Ti–N bound: as a result silicon content is less than 10 at% and the film is not amorphous. An important issue for applications is to preserve stable TiSiN electrical property. The microstructure and the degree of oxidation are key parameters to control it [13,14] . Densification and crystallisation of the TiSiN films were observed for short thermal treatments at temperatures higher than 800   ∘   C  , resulting in a lower electrical resistivity. A strong modification of the film structure and density is found after PECVD deposition of a SiO  2     layer at 550   ∘   C  , as a consequence of the TiSiN oxidation. This is consistent with the negative free Gibbs energy of reaction of TiN with oxygen ( TiN + O  2   ⇒ TiO  2   + N  2   )  , that was calculated at - 545  kJ / mol   at 425   ∘   C  , and - 504  kJ / mol   at 900   ∘   C  . The weak oxidation resistance poses some constraints on the process integration of the material because TiSiN oxidation results in a strong increase of the electrical resistivity.
